Who's affected

It's not only artists.
It's everyone who ever put something online.

Generative AI is trained on the open web. That sounds abstract until you realise the open web means your selfies, your mother's voice note, the sketch you posted in eighth grade, the reel you filmed last Tuesday. Here are five ways that plays out.

01 · Ordinary users

Your photos, their deepfakes.

Every public photo is a training sample. Face-swap apps, undressing tools, and non-consensual deepfake porn all rely on models that learned from ordinary selfies — most of which were never meant for that purpose. Once an image leaves your phone, it can be reconstituted into something you never posed for.

e.g., reporting on deepfake harassment in Indian schools and colleges, 2023–24.

02 · Voters

Audio and video you can't trust.

Cloned political voices and AI-generated rally footage have already circulated in elections in India, the US, Slovakia, and Bangladesh. Detection tools lag behind generation tools by months, sometimes years. The cost of making a convincing fake dropped from a studio to a laptop and ten minutes.

e.g., AI-generated audio of political leaders across Indian state elections, 2024.

03 · Artists

Style mimicry isn't flattery — it's replacement.

A diffusion model that has seen a few dozen of an illustrator's pieces can generate new work in their style on demand, for free, in seconds. Commissions dry up. Clients pick the $0 version. The artist's own search results get buried under imitations. Class-action lawsuits are slow; by the time courts rule, the career damage is done.

e.g., the Glaze user study — 1,156 artists, 88% want a protection tool.

04 · Creators

AI translation and the missing original.

Platforms now auto-dub creators into languages they don't speak, matching lip movements and voice timbre. Nuance, slang, and regional identity get flattened. More importantly: the AI-modified version often gets more reach than the original — so the original effectively disappears.

e.g., a Tamil creator's joke, re-dubbed into Hindi, becomes a different joke.

05 · Everyone

You can't read what you can't find.

Most platforms bury AI-training clauses inside 40-page Terms of Service that the average person will never read — studies repeatedly show under 10% do. Opt out of AI training is either missing, hidden three menus deep, or only available to users in specific jurisdictions.

Consent, in practice, is manufactured by exhaustion.

The collateral damage: a public record that's closing itself off.

The pushback from publishers isn't aimed only at OpenAI or Anthropic. When a newspaper updates its robots.txt to keep AI crawlers out, the same rule frequently locks out the Internet Archive too — the public-memory service that has preserved over a trillion web pages since 1996. By late 2024, more than half of the world's top news sites were blocking at least one major AI crawler; many also block ia_archiver by association. Journalism and the public record used to share one infrastructure. AI-training anxiety is pulling them apart.

The bind is brutal: the same tools publishers reach for to keep AI models out also keep librarians, researchers, and ordinary readers from preserving what was published. The remedy we build for one problem is quietly dismantling the piece of the web most of us assumed would always be there.

India in focus

India has 800 million internet users
and one of the world's weakest consent floors.

Most global AI-consent discourse is framed around GDPR and the EU AI Act. Indian users rarely see themselves in it.

India is one of the largest sources of training data on the planet — photos, voices, languages, faces, handwritten scripts. It is also, for now, one of the jurisdictions where AI companies face the least friction. The EU can fine a company billions under the AI Act. A user in Berlin can demand their data be deleted under GDPR. A user in Bengaluru, in most cases, cannot.

The regulatory gap

Jurisdiction Key law What it gives users
European Union GDPR (2018) + AI Act (2024) Right to erasure, right to object to automated processing, fines up to 7% of global revenue.
California, USA CCPA / CPRA Right to know, delete, opt out of sale or sharing — including for AI training.
India DPDP Act, 2023 Consent-based framework on paper; rules and Data Protection Board still being operationalised. No AI-specific provisions.
United Kingdom Data Protection Act + emerging AI regulation Similar to GDPR; still evolving.

Tested on India first

Several patterns recur in reporting on AI deployment in India:

  • Features launched in India before the EU or US, because regulatory review is faster and consent requirements looser.
  • Voice and facial datasets collected via free apps marketed heavily in Indian languages, later used to train models sold globally.
  • Low-wage data annotation and RLHF work subcontracted to Indian workers labelling violent or disturbing content without adequate psychological support — documented by Time, The Verge, and Rest of World over 2022–24.

The pattern isn't conspiracy — it's economic gravity. Where the regulatory cost of a mistake is low and the labour cost of data work is low, companies go. India is currently both.

What creators in India can do today

Know your DPDP rights.

The Act gives you the right to consent, purpose limitation, and erasure. It is narrow, and it is still rolling out — but it is what you have.

Use the toolbox below.

Glaze, Nightshade, and C2PA work regardless of jurisdiction. Laws can wait; your images can't.

Document exposure.

If your work or likeness appears in a scraped dataset, there's a growing evidence base — e.g. haveibeentrained.com.

The Toolbox

You don't have to wait for a law.
These three tools exist now.

None of them are silver bullets. Each addresses a different layer of the problem — proving what something is, hiding what a model can learn from it, and poisoning what a model tries to steal. Used together, they shift leverage back to the creator.

C2PA

A tamper-evident receipt for every piece of media.

An open standard — backed by Adobe, Microsoft, BBC, Nikon, Leica and ~300 others — that attaches a cryptographically signed manifest to your file. It records who made it, when, with what tool, and whether AI was involved.

Deep dive →

Glaze

A cloak for your art style.

From the University of Chicago's SAND Lab. Nearly-invisible perturbations that change nothing for a human eye but make a diffusion model learn the wrong style. Defensive. Over 93% effective in the authors' study of 1,156 artists.

Deep dive →

Nightshade

If you scrape me, you poison your model.

From the same lab — but on offence. It modifies an image so that, to a human, it still looks like a dog — but to a model training on it, "dog" starts to mean "cat." As few as 50–100 samples measurably corrupts a concept.

What artists actually think

The people closest to the problem
aren't waiting for permission anymore.

Researchers at UChicago's SAND Lab surveyed 1,156 professional artists — illustrators, concept artists, animators — for the Glaze paper (USENIX Security 2023). It is, to date, the largest structured record of what working creators actually feel about generative AI training on their work. The numbers are not ambivalent.

77% style already mimicked
+
24% income already hit

This isn't a forecast. It's already happened.

77% of surveyed artists confirmed that generative models successfully mimic their specific style — not a generic "fantasy illustration" look, but something a regular client would recognise as theirs. A quarter (24%) report that this mimicry has already cost them paid work. The loss isn't hypothetical and isn't in the future tense.

88% want a protection tool
→
93% rated Glaze successful

The demand is clear. So is the early verdict on the tool.

88% of respondents said they wanted a dedicated protection tool against style mimicry before one existed. After trying Glaze, 93% rated it successful at disrupting that mimicry. The appetite for a technical opt-out isn't niche or fringe — it's nearly the entire working profession, and the first credible answer clears the bar.

89% taking defensive action
+
50% considering going dark

When the argument can't arrive in time, self-defence becomes the argument.

89% of artists have already taken, or plan to take, defensive measures: watermarking, style-cloaking, locking portfolios, leaving platforms. Over half are actively considering removing their work from the public internet entirely — the creative-economy equivalent of pulling out of the public square because the square no longer protects them.

51% willing to sue

Litigation is no longer a fringe position.

51% of surveyed artists said they would personally join a class-action lawsuit against an AI company training on their work without consent. In a profession historically reluctant to litigate — freelancers, small studios, long careers built on client goodwill — a clean majority willing to go to court is a structural shift, not a handful of outliers.

Methodology note

Source: Shan et al., Glaze: Protecting Artists from Style Mimicry by Text-to-Image Models, USENIX Security 2023. 1,156 professional artists surveyed, recruited through artist community networks — predominantly full-time (46%) or part-time / freelance (50%), across illustration, concept art, animation, and related fields.

The survey is not a random sample of all artists; it skews towards creators already aware of AI-training concerns and engaged enough to respond. Read the figures as the view from the affected, attentive core of the profession — the people who will define what the rest eventually demand.
